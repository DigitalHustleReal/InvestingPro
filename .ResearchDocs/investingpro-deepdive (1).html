One specific question per article — not broad topics
The core content strategy that beat every major bank
36,000 pages ranked
The breakthrough insight: Google doesn't rank articles about "credit cards." It ranks articles that answer one specific query better than anyone else. NerdWallet's VP of Content said: "Our content strategy is writing for a consumer with a very specific problem." This meant: instead of "best travel credit cards," they wrote "best credit card for Delta Sky Miles under $100 annual fee." Each article was so specific that it had almost no competition — and converted better because the reader felt understood.
Exactly how they did it — replicable step by step
1
Listed every possible modifier: by age, spend level, credit score range, merchant category, city, income bracket, lifestyle (student, freelancer, etc.)

4
Internal linking: every article linked to the pillar page + 3–5 related articles. Users stayed on-site. Google rewarded the time-on-site signal.
5
Updated each article every 6 months with new data. "Last reviewed: [date]" signals freshness to Google.

The Credit Karma flywheel — how "free" became $7 billion

"Scores were built for lenders and statisticians. Consumers were not the intended audience." — Ken Lin, Credit Karma founder
2007
The founding insight: make credit scores free — actually free
At the time, every "free credit score" service was a bait-and-switch — they'd charge $19.99/month subscription after getting your details. Ken Lin's insight: if you give the score away genuinely free, you learn the user's credit profile. If you know the profile, you can match them with products they'll actually get approved for. Banks would pay for qualified leads. The user gets free scores. Banks get better conversion. Credit Karma captures the spread.
First genuinely free credit score in the US. Immediate differentiation.
2008
TransUnion sent a cease-and-desist. Near-death experience.
Credit Karma's only data source threatened to cut them off. Ken nearly shut the company. Instead, he negotiated — demonstrating that free scores actually increased TransUnion data quality by bringing in previously unscored consumers. Lesson: product-market fit so strong that even existential threats get resolved in your favour.
Survived. Accelerated. 1M scores given away within 2 years.
2009
First VC raise: $2.5M. Then didn't raise again for 4 years.
Ken was profitable early. "We were profitable pretty early — that was one of our secrets." The free score model generated qualified lead data worth more to banks than they were charging. They ran lean. Bootstrapped mentality even with VC money. Didn't raise again until $30M in 2013 from Ribbit Capital, by which point they had tens of millions of users.
Profitable while giving away free product. Rare. The data was the revenue.
2012–14
Personalised product recommendations based on credit profile
Credit Karma's secret weapon: they knew every user's exact credit score, utilisation, payment history, account age, and enquiry count. This meant they could show: "Based on your score, you have a 91% approval chance for this card." Conversion rates were 3–5x higher than generic comparison sites because recommendations were personalised. Banks paid premium CPAs for these qualified leads.
Revenue per user far higher than NerdWallet or Bankrate. Data moat.
2015
$500 TV ad broke the growth curve — "approved offers" feature
Ken bought a $500 local TV spot. Unexpected response. Then scaled TV. But the bigger breakthrough was "approved offers" — products Credit Karma knew with high confidence the user would be approved for, based on soft-pull credit data. Users applied with confidence. Banks got lower rejection rates. Credit Karma's commission per successful application surged.
110M members. Largest personal finance app in the US.
2020
Intuit acquired for $7.1B. The credit profile data was the acquisition price.
Intuit (TurboTax, QuickBooks) paid $7.1B primarily for Credit Karma's financial profile data on 110M Americans. The combination: tax data (Intuit) + credit data (Credit Karma) = the most complete picture of American consumer finances ever assembled.
