# How to Start Investing in Stocks in India — Complete Beginner Guide 2026

You have heard it a hundred times: *"Invest in the stock market."* Your colleague made money on Tata Motors, your cousin doubled his money on Reliance, and every finance influencer on Instagram is showing off green portfolios. But when you actually open a broker app, you freeze. What to buy? How to buy? What if you lose everything?

This guide is for you — a complete beginner sitting in India, wanting to put real money into stocks but not sure where to start. No jargon walls. No complicated charts. Just clear, step-by-step instructions written the way your smart friend would explain it over chai.

By the end, you will know how to open a demat account, buy your first stock, read basic numbers, and — most importantly — avoid the costly mistakes that trip up 90% of beginners.

## Key Takeaways

- You need a demat account + trading account to buy stocks in India — opening one takes 15 minutes online
- Start with large, well-known companies (Nifty 50 stocks) — not penny stocks
- Never invest money you might need in the next 2-3 years
- Long-term capital gains (LTCG) above Rs 1.25 lakh are taxed at 12.5%; short-term (STCG) at 20%
- Learning 4 basic ratios (PE, EPS, Debt-to-Equity, ROE) gives you an edge over 80% of retail investors

## 1. What Are Stocks and Why Should You Invest in Them?

A stock (also called a share or equity) is a tiny piece of ownership in a company. When you buy 10 shares of Infosys, you literally own a small part of Infosys. If the company does well, your shares become more valuable. If it pays a dividend, you get a cash payout every year.

### Why Stocks Beat Other Investments Over the Long Term

Let us compare how Rs 1 lakh invested in 2015 would have grown by 2025:

| Investment | Approx. Value in 2025 | Annualized Return |
|------------|----------------------|-------------------|
| Fixed Deposit (SBI) | Rs 1.72 lakh | ~5.5% |
| Gold | Rs 2.40 lakh | ~9.2% |
| Nifty 50 (Index) | Rs 2.95 lakh | ~11.4% |
| Top mutual fund (Flexi cap) | Rs 3.50 lakh | ~13.4% |

Stocks have consistently beaten inflation, FDs, and even gold over 10+ year periods. The key word is **long term**. In the short term (weeks or months), stock prices jump around like crazy. Over 7-10 years, quality stocks almost always go up.

### Who Should Invest in Stocks?

- Anyone with a stable income and an emergency fund (3-6 months of expenses saved)
- People who can stay invested for at least 3-5 years
- Anyone who wants their money to beat inflation (FD rates barely cover it)

### Who Should NOT Invest (Yet)?

- If you have high-interest debt (personal loans, credit card dues) — pay that off first
- If you do not have an emergency fund — build that first
- If you need this money within 1-2 years (for a wedding, car, etc.) — use FDs or debt mutual funds instead

## 2. Demat Account and Trading Account — Your Gateway to the Stock Market

Before you can buy a single share, you need two accounts:

### Demat Account (Dematerialized Account)

Think of it as a digital locker that holds your shares. Just like a bank account holds your money, a demat account holds your stocks in electronic form. Before 1996, people held physical share certificates — paper documents that could get lost, torn, or forged. The demat system fixed all of that.

Your demat account is maintained by one of two depositories in India:
- **NSDL** (National Securities Depository Limited)
- **CDSL** (Central Depository Services Limited)

### Trading Account

This is the account you use to actually place buy and sell orders on the stock exchange. Your trading account is linked to your demat account and your bank account. Money flows like this:

**Bank Account → Trading Account → Stock Exchange → Shares land in Demat Account**

### How to Open a Demat + Trading Account (2026)

Most brokers today open both accounts together — you do not need to go to separate places. The entire process is online and takes 15-20 minutes.

#### Documents You Need

1. **PAN Card** — mandatory (your PAN is your identity in the financial world)
2. **Aadhaar Card** — for e-KYC (online verification)
3. **Bank account details** — a cancelled cheque or bank statement
4. **Signature** — digital or photo on white paper
5. **Selfie/photo** — for verification

#### Step-by-Step Account Opening (Generic Process)

1. Go to the broker's website or app (e.g., zerodha.com, groww.in)
2. Enter your mobile number — you will get an OTP

5. Upload your signature photo
6. Take a selfie for verification
7. E-sign the application using Aadhaar
8. Done — your account is usually active within 24-48 hours

> **Pro Tip:** Your PAN must be linked to your Aadhaar. If it is not, do it on the Income Tax portal first — otherwise your account opening will get stuck.

## 3. BSE vs NSE — The Two Stock Exchanges in India

India has two major stock exchanges where stocks are bought and sold:

| Feature | BSE (Bombay Stock Exchange) | NSE (National Stock Exchange) |
|---------|---------------------------|------------------------------|
| Founded | 1875 (oldest in Asia) | 1992 |
| Benchmark Index | Sensex (30 stocks) | Nifty 50 (50 stocks) |
| Listed Companies | 5,500+ | 2,200+ |
| Trading Volume | Lower | Higher (most active) |
| Derivatives (F&O) | Available but less liquid | Dominant for F&O |

### Which One Should You Use?

As a beginner, you do not need to worry about this. Most large companies are listed on **both** exchanges, and the prices are almost identical. When you place an order on your broker app (Zerodha, Groww), the broker routes it to whichever exchange gives you the best price. You just click "Buy" and the system handles the rest.

If you ever see two options (BSE and NSE) while buying a stock, **pick NSE** — it has higher trading volume, which means your order gets filled faster and at a fair price.

## 4. How to Buy Your First Stock — Step-by-Step

Let us walk through buying a stock on a typical broker app. We will use Zerodha's Kite as the example, but Groww and Angel One work similarly.

### Before You Buy: Add Money to Your Trading Account

1. Open your broker app
2. Go to "Funds" or "Add Money"
3. Enter the amount (start with Rs 5,000-10,000)
4. Pay via UPI or net banking — money shows up in your trading account instantly

### Buying Your First Stock

1. **Search for the company** — Type "Reliance" or "TCS" in the search bar
2. **Select the stock** — You will see the full name, current price, and exchange (NSE/BSE). Pick NSE.
3. **Tap "Buy" or "B"** — A buy order form opens
4. **Choose order type** — For your first trade, use a **Market order** (explained in detail below)
5. **Enter quantity** — How many shares you want. If TCS is Rs 4,000/share and you have Rs 10,000, you can buy 2 shares.
6. **Select "Delivery"** (not Intraday) — This means you are buying the stock to hold, not to sell the same day
7. **Review the order** — Check the total amount, taxes, and brokerage
8. **Swipe to confirm** — Your order goes to the exchange
9. **Order gets executed** — Within seconds for liquid stocks
10. **Shares appear in your demat account** — After T+1 settlement (shares are credited the next trading day)

**Congratulations!** You are now a shareholder. You own a tiny piece of a real company. The stock will sit in your demat account until you decide to sell it — could be 1 month, 1 year, or 20 years.

## 5. Types of Stock Orders: Market, Limit, and Stop-Loss

When you buy or sell a stock, you need to tell the exchange *how* you want the trade executed. There are three main order types:

### Market Order

**What it means:** "Buy/sell this stock right now, at whatever the current price is."

A market order gets executed almost instantly. You do not choose the exact price — you get the best available price at that moment. For popular stocks like Reliance or HDFC Bank, the price you see and the price you get will be nearly identical.

**When to use:** When you want to buy or sell quickly and do not care about a few paise difference.

**Risk:** For low-volume stocks (small companies), the execution price might be slightly different from what you saw — this is called **slippage**.

### Limit Order

**What it means:** "Buy this stock only if the price drops to Rs X" or "Sell only if it rises to Rs X."

You set a specific price. The order will only execute if the stock reaches that price. If it never reaches your price, the order expires at the end of the trading day.

**Example:** Infosys is trading at Rs 1,600. You place a limit buy order at Rs 1,570. If the price drops to Rs 1,570 during the day, your order gets filled. If it does not, the order cancels at 3:30 PM.

**When to use:** When you have a target price in mind and are okay with not getting the stock today.

### Stop-Loss Order

**What it means:** "If the price drops to Rs X, sell my shares automatically to limit my loss."

A stop-loss is your safety net. Let us say you bought a stock at Rs 500. You set a stop-loss at Rs 450. If the price drops to Rs 450, the system automatically sells your shares — you lose Rs 50 per share instead of potentially Rs 200 if the stock crashes further.

**Example:** You buy Tata Steel at Rs 160. You set a stop-loss at Rs 145. If Tata Steel drops to Rs 145, your shares are sold automatically. Maximum loss = Rs 15 per share (about 9.4%).

**When to use:** Always. Seriously — every trade should have a stop-loss, especially for beginners.

| Order Type | Speed | Price Control | Best For |
|------------|-------|---------------|----------|
| Market | Instant | None (market decides) | Quick buy/sell of popular stocks |
| Limit | Only when price matches | Full (you set the price) | Buying at a specific target price |
| Stop-Loss | Triggers at your price | Partial | Protecting against big losses |

## 6. Fundamental Analysis Basics — How to Judge if a Stock Is Worth Buying

Fundamental analysis means looking at a company's financial health to decide if its stock is fairly priced, overpriced, or underpriced. You do not need an MBA for this. Understanding four simple ratios puts you ahead of most retail investors in India.

### PE Ratio (Price-to-Earnings Ratio)

**What it tells you:** How much investors are willing to pay for every Rs 1 of the company's earnings.

**Formula:** PE = Stock Price / Earnings Per Share (EPS)

**Example:** If a stock is priced at Rs 600 and its EPS is Rs 30, the PE ratio is 600 / 30 = 20. This means investors are paying Rs 20 for every Rs 1 the company earns.

**How to read it:**
- PE of 10-15 — Generally considered undervalued (cheap)
- PE of 15-25 — Fairly valued for growing companies
- PE of 25-40 — Expensive, but might be justified for fast-growing companies (IT, FMCG)
- PE above 50 — Very expensive. Proceed with caution.

**Important:** Always compare PE within the same sector. An IT company with PE of 30 is normal. A PSU bank with PE of 30 is expensive. Compare Infosys with TCS, not with SBI.

### EPS (Earnings Per Share)

**What it tells you:** How much profit the company makes per share.

**Formula:** EPS = Net Profit / Total Number of Shares

**Example:** If a company made Rs 5,000 crore profit and has 100 crore shares, EPS = Rs 50.

**How to use it:** Look at EPS growth over 3-5 years. A company whose EPS is growing by 15-20% every year is doing well. If EPS is flat or declining, the company might be in trouble — even if the stock price is going up (that is a red flag).

### Debt-to-Equity Ratio

**What it tells you:** How much debt the company has compared to its own money (equity).

**Formula:** D/E = Total Debt / Shareholder Equity

**Example:** If a company has Rs 2,000 crore debt and Rs 4,000 crore equity, D/E = 0.5

**How to read it:**
- D/E below 0.5 — Very low debt (strong balance sheet)
- D/E of 0.5-1.0 — Manageable debt
- D/E of 1.0-2.0 — High debt (acceptable only for capital-intensive sectors like infra, power)
- D/E above 2.0 — Dangerous for most companies

**Pro Tip:** Companies with low debt survive recessions better. During COVID (2020), many highly-indebted companies crashed 70-80%, while low-debt companies recovered fast.

### ROE (Return on Equity)

**What it tells you:** How efficiently the company uses shareholder money to generate profit.

**Formula:** ROE = Net Profit / Shareholder Equity × 100

**Example:** If a company has equity of Rs 1,000 crore and net profit of Rs 200 crore, ROE = 20%.

**How to read it:**
- ROE above 20% — Excellent (companies like TCS, HUL consistently deliver this)
- ROE of 15-20% — Good
- ROE of 10-15% — Average
- ROE below 10% — The company is not using its capital efficiently

> **The Quick Stock Screener:** When looking at any stock, check these four numbers: PE ratio (is it fairly priced?), EPS growth (is the company growing?), D/E ratio (is debt under control?), ROE (is management efficient?). If all four look good, the stock is worth researching further.

## 7. How to Read a Stock Page — Understanding the Numbers

When you open any stock on Zerodha, Groww, Moneycontrol, or Google, you see a bunch of numbers. Here is what each one means:

### Current Price (CMP — Current Market Price)

The last traded price. This is what one share costs right now. It changes every second during market hours (9:15 AM to 3:30 PM, Monday to Friday).

### Day Change (e.g., +2.5% or -1.3%)

How much the price has moved today compared to yesterday's closing price. Green means up, red means down.

### Volume

The number of shares traded today. Higher volume means more people are buying and selling — the stock is **liquid**. For beginners, always invest in high-volume stocks (above 1 lakh shares/day). Low-volume stocks are hard to sell when you need to.

### 52-Week High and Low

The highest and lowest price the stock reached in the last one year. This gives you perspective:
- If a stock is near its 52-week high, it might be expensive (or on a strong uptrend)
- If it is near the 52-week low, it might be cheap (or in trouble — find out why)

**Never buy a stock just because it is at a 52-week low.** Sometimes stocks are cheap for a reason (bad earnings, scam, industry decline). Do your research first.

### Market Capitalization (Market Cap)

The total value of the company = Stock Price × Total Number of Shares.

Companies are categorized by market cap:

| Category | Market Cap | Examples | Risk Level |
|----------|-----------|----------|------------|
| Large Cap | Above Rs 20,000 crore | Reliance, TCS, HDFC Bank, Infosys | Lower |
| Mid Cap | Rs 5,000-20,000 crore | Voltas, Trent, Indian Hotels | Medium |
| Small Cap | Below Rs 5,000 crore | Varies widely | Higher |

**For beginners:** Stick to large-cap stocks. They are more stable, better researched, and less likely to give you heart attacks.

### Other Numbers You Will See

- **Open:** The price at which the stock started trading today
- **Close/Prev Close:** Yesterday's closing price
- **Upper Circuit / Lower Circuit:** The maximum a stock can rise or fall in one day (SEBI sets these limits)
- **Face Value:** The original value of one share when the company issued it (usually Rs 1, 2, 5, or 10). Not important for trading.
- **Dividend Yield:** Annual dividend as a percentage of the stock price. A yield of 2-3% means decent passive income.

## 8. Nifty 50 and Sensex — India's Market Barometers

You will hear these two terms every single day if you follow financial news. They are **stock market indices** — think of them as the report card of the Indian stock market.

### Sensex (BSE)

The **S&P BSE Sensex** tracks the top 30 companies listed on the Bombay Stock Exchange by market cap. It was introduced in 1986 and is the oldest index in India.

**Some Sensex companies:** Reliance, TCS, HDFC Bank, Infosys, ITC, Hindustan Unilever, Bajaj Finance, L&T

### Nifty 50 (NSE)

The **Nifty 50** tracks the top 50 companies listed on the National Stock Exchange. It was introduced in 1996 and is the more commonly followed index today.

**Nifty 50 includes all Sensex companies** plus 20 more large companies.

### What Do These Numbers Mean?

When the news says "Nifty crossed 25,000" or "Sensex hit 82,000," it means the weighted average value of those top companies has reached that level. The actual number is just a score — what matters is the **percentage change**.

- "Nifty fell 2% today" — means the top 50 companies lost about 2% of their value on average
- "Sensex gained 500 points" — sounds dramatic, but at 80,000 that is only 0.6%

### Why Should You Care?

- **Market health indicator:** If Nifty is falling consistently, the overall market is bearish. If it is rising, the market is bullish.
- **Benchmark for your portfolio:** If your portfolio made 8% but Nifty made 15%, you underperformed. You could have just bought a Nifty index fund instead.
- **Index investing:** You can invest directly in Nifty 50 through index funds or ETFs — this is actually the simplest way to invest in stocks (more on this below).

> **Beginner Strategy:** If picking individual stocks feels overwhelming, start with a Nifty 50 index fund (like UTI Nifty 50 Index Fund or Nippon India Nifty 50 BeES ETF). You get instant diversification across 50 top Indian companies. Warren Buffett himself recommends index funds for most people.

## 9. Intraday vs Delivery Trading — Which One Is for Beginners?

When you buy a stock, you have two options on your broker app: **Intraday** and **Delivery** (also called CNC or Longterm).

### Delivery Trading (CNC)

**What it means:** You buy the stock and it gets delivered to your demat account. You can hold it for as long as you want — days, months, years, decades.

- Shares are credited to your demat account after T+1 (next trading day)
- Brokerage is zero on most platforms (Zerodha, Groww, Angel One)
- No leverage — you can only buy stocks worth the cash in your account
- You become a real part-owner of the company

### Intraday Trading (MIS)

**What it means:** You buy and sell the stock on the same day, before market close at 3:30 PM. You never actually own the shares — you are just betting on the price movement within the day.

- If you do not sell by 3:15-3:20 PM, the broker auto-squares off your position
- You get **leverage** (margin) — with Rs 10,000, you can trade stocks worth Rs 50,000-1,00,000
- Brokerage is Rs 20 per order on most platforms
- Very high risk — studies show 90%+ of intraday traders lose money

| Feature | Delivery (CNC) | Intraday (MIS) |
|---------|----------------|----------------|
| Holding period | Unlimited | Same day only |
| Ownership | Yes (shares in demat) | No (just price speculation) |
| Brokerage | Zero (most brokers) | Rs 20/order |
| Leverage | None (1x) | 5x to 10x |
| Risk level | Lower | Very high |
| SEBI data on profitability | Most investors profit (long-term) | 93% lose money (SEBI 2024 study) |

> **Strong Warning:** As a beginner, do NOT do intraday trading. This is not investing — it is speculation. A SEBI study (January 2024) found that **93% of individual intraday traders in India lost money** over a 3-year period, with average losses of Rs 2 lakh per person. Start with delivery trading and hold good stocks for 1-5+ years.

## 10. Tax on Stock Market Gains in India (FY 2025-26)

Yes, you have to pay tax on stock profits. The rates depend on how long you held the stock.

### Short-Term Capital Gains (STCG)

**Applies when:** You sell a stock within 12 months of buying it.
**Tax rate:** **20%** on the profit (changed from 15% in Budget 2024)

**Example:** You buy a stock at Rs 100 in January 2026 and sell at Rs 150 in June 2026. Profit = Rs 50 per share. Tax = Rs 50 × 20% = Rs 10 per share.

### Long-Term Capital Gains (LTCG)

**Applies when:** You sell a stock after holding it for more than 12 months.
**Tax rate:** **12.5%** on profit exceeding Rs 1.25 lakh per financial year (changed from 10% above Rs 1 lakh in Budget 2024)

**Example:** You buy stocks worth Rs 5 lakh in April 2025 and sell them for Rs 7 lakh in May 2026. Profit = Rs 2 lakh. Tax-free portion = Rs 1.25 lakh. Taxable = Rs 75,000. Tax = Rs 75,000 × 12.5% = Rs 9,375.

### Other Charges You Will Pay

| Charge | Rate | When |
|--------|------|------|
| STT (Securities Transaction Tax) | 0.1% on buy + sell (delivery) | Every transaction |
| GST on brokerage | 18% of brokerage amount | Every transaction |
| Stamp Duty | 0.015% on buy side | Every buy |
| Exchange charges | ~0.00345% | Every transaction |
| SEBI turnover fee | 0.0001% | Every transaction |
| DP charges | Rs 15-18 per sell transaction | When selling from demat |

> **Tax-Saving Tip:** If your total LTCG from stocks and equity mutual funds is under Rs 1.25 lakh in a year, you pay ZERO tax. For most beginners with small portfolios, this means no tax on long-term gains for several years.

### Dividend Tax

Dividends received from stocks are added to your total income and taxed at your income tax slab rate. If you are in the 30% tax bracket, you pay 30% tax on dividends. This changed in 2020 — earlier, dividends were tax-free in the hands of investors.

## 11. Ten Common Beginner Mistakes (and How to Avoid Them)

Every new investor makes mistakes. The goal is to make small mistakes, learn fast, and not blow up your capital. Here are the ten most common traps:

### Mistake 1: Buying Penny Stocks

**What happens:** You see a stock at Rs 2 and think "If it goes to Rs 20, I will make 10x!" So you buy 10,000 shares for Rs 20,000.

**Reality:** Most penny stocks are penny stocks for a reason — they are low-quality companies with no real business. Many are operator-driven (manipulated by insiders), and when the price crashes, there is no one to sell to because volume dries up.

**Fix:** Stick to Nifty 50 or Nifty Next 50 companies for your first year. Boring is beautiful in investing.

### Mistake 2: Not Using Stop-Loss

**What happens:** You buy a stock at Rs 500. It drops to Rs 400. You think "It will come back." It drops to Rs 300. Then Rs 200. You are now sitting on a 60% loss and too scared to sell.

**Fix:** Set a stop-loss at 10-15% below your buy price for every stock. If it hits, sell and move on. Protecting your capital is more important than being right.

### Mistake 3: Following Tips from WhatsApp Groups and Telegram Channels

**What happens:** Someone in a group says "XYZ stock will double in 2 weeks." You buy. The stock falls 30%. The person who gave the tip already sold at a profit (because they bought before telling you).

**Fix:** Never buy a stock based on someone else's recommendation without doing your own research. If someone had a guaranteed stock tip, why would they share it with 10,000 strangers for free?

### Mistake 4: Investing Money You Cannot Afford to Lose

**What happens:** You invest your emergency fund or money meant for rent because "the market is going up." Then the market corrects 15% and you are forced to sell at a loss because you need the money.

**Fix:** Only invest money you will not need for at least 3-5 years. Keep 3-6 months of expenses in a savings account or liquid fund before investing in stocks.

### Mistake 5: Checking Your Portfolio Every Hour

**What happens:** You bought a stock yesterday. Today it is down 1.5%. You panic. Tomorrow it is up 0.5%. You feel euphoric. This emotional rollercoaster leads to panic selling and impulsive buying.

**Fix:** Check your portfolio once a week, or even once a month. Daily price movements are noise. What matters is the company's performance over quarters and years.

### Mistake 6: Putting All Money in One Stock

**What happens:** You are convinced Adani Green is the future. You put Rs 5 lakh — all your savings — in one stock. It drops 40% in a month.

**Fix:** Diversify across at least 8-12 stocks in different sectors. Never put more than 10-15% of your portfolio in any single stock. If picking 12 stocks feels overwhelming, buy a Nifty 50 index fund — instant diversification.

### Mistake 7: Trying to Time the Market

**What happens:** You wait for the "perfect dip" to buy. The market keeps going up. You wait more. Eventually you buy at a much higher price out of FOMO, and then the market corrects.

**Fix:** Time in the market beats timing the market. Start with a small amount and invest regularly (like a monthly SIP in stocks). Over 10 years, whether you bought at the top or bottom barely matters.

### Mistake 8: Ignoring Fundamentals and Chasing Momentum

**What happens:** A stock has gone up 50% in 2 months. Everyone on Twitter is talking about it. You buy at the peak. The stock corrects 30% in the next month.

**Fix:** Always check the PE ratio, EPS growth, and debt before buying. A stock going up does not mean it will keep going up. Price follows earnings over the long term — not hype.

### Mistake 9: Not Having a Clear Goal

**What happens:** You "invest" Rs 50,000 without knowing why. Is it for retirement? A house down payment? A vacation? Without a goal, you do not know when to sell or how much risk to take.

**Fix:** Define your goal before investing. "I am investing Rs 10,000/month in large-cap stocks for 10 years for my child's education" is a real plan. "I want to make money in the stock market" is not.

### Mistake 10: Averaging Down Without Reason

**What happens:** You buy a stock at Rs 200. It drops to Rs 150. You buy more to "reduce your average cost." It drops to Rs 100. You buy even more. Now you have a huge position in a falling stock.

**Fix:** Only average down if the company's fundamentals are intact and you genuinely believe the stock is undervalued. If the stock is falling because the company is doing poorly (declining revenue, rising debt, management issues), buying more is like catching a falling knife. Cut your losses instead.

Here is a realistic plan for your first three months as a stock investor:

### Week 1-2: Setup
- Open a demat + trading account (Zerodha or Groww)
- Link your bank account
- Add Rs 5,000-10,000 to start

- Buy 1-2 shares of a Nifty 50 company you understand (Reliance, TCS, HDFC Bank, ITC)

### Month 2: Build Knowledge
- Learn to read company results (quarterly earnings)
- Check PE, EPS, D/E, and ROE for 10 companies
- Start a Nifty 50 index fund SIP of Rs 2,000/month
- Follow CNBC-TV18 or Moneycontrol for market news (but do not react to daily noise)

### Month 3: Expand Gradually
- Buy 2-3 more stocks across different sectors (IT, banking, FMCG, pharma)
- Build a portfolio of 5-8 stocks + 1 index fund
- Track your portfolio on the broker app or a spreadsheet
- Review your stop-losses and adjust if needed

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much money do I need to start investing in stocks?

You can start with as little as Rs 500-1,000. Many stocks are priced under Rs 500 per share. Even buying 1 share of a company like ITC (around Rs 430) is a valid start. The important thing is to begin — you can increase your investments as you earn more and learn more.

### Is the stock market safe for beginners?

The stock market is not "safe" or "unsafe" — it depends entirely on how you invest. Buying large-cap stocks and holding for 5+ years is historically quite safe. Day trading with leverage is extremely risky. The market itself is well-regulated by SEBI. Your money in a demat account is as safe as money in a bank account.

### What is the best time to buy stocks?

There is no perfect time. Studies show that investing consistently (every month) beats trying to time the market. That said, market dips (10-15% corrections) are good opportunities to buy quality stocks at a discount. The worst time to buy is when everyone is euphoric and stocks are at all-time highs.

### Can I lose all my money in stocks?

If you buy a diversified portfolio of large-cap stocks and hold for 5+ years, losing all your money is virtually impossible. You would need every company in your portfolio to go bankrupt simultaneously, which has never happened with Nifty 50 companies. However, individual stocks can lose 50-90% of their value if the company fails — which is why diversification is essential.

### What is the difference between stocks and mutual funds?

When you buy a stock, you pick and buy shares of one company yourself. In a mutual fund, a professional fund manager pools money from thousands of investors and buys stocks on their behalf. Mutual funds give you diversification and professional management but charge an expense ratio (0.5-2% per year). If you do not have the time or interest to pick stocks, mutual funds (especially index funds) are the better choice.

### Do I need to file income tax returns for stock investments?

Yes. If you have capital gains from stocks (even if they are below the tax-free limit), you should report them in your ITR. Your broker provides a tax P&L statement (usually in the "Reports" or "Console" section) that has all the details you need. Most people use ITR-2 or ITR-3 forms for reporting capital gains.

### What happens if my broker shuts down?

Your shares are held in your demat account at CDSL or NSDL — not with the broker. If your broker shuts down (like Karvy did in 2019), your shares are still safe. You simply transfer your demat account to another broker. This is why the demat system exists — to protect investors.

### Should I invest in stocks or mutual funds as a beginner?

If you have 30 minutes a week to research companies and enjoy learning about businesses, start with stocks. If you want a hands-off approach, go with mutual funds (Nifty 50 index fund is the best starting point). Many smart investors do both — index fund SIP for the core portfolio + a few individual stocks for learning and potentially higher returns.
